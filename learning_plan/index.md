# Learning Plan (2026–2027)

Amateur radio is full of rabbit holes, and my approach is to explore them at my own pace, letting each experiment and observation guide the next. I’m less focused on “hitting milestones” and more on seeing how things actually work and connecting theory to practice.

## Playing and Operating

- Log QSOs across bands and modes, notice patterns, and see what setups make communication easier.
- Hang out on weekly nets or with local clubs, listening, trying different roles, and learning from how others operate.
- Experiment with antennas:
  - Build a 2 m Yagi, test it, move it around, compare predicted vs. real performance.
  - See how placement, orientation, and environment change what I hear and reach.
- Slowly build a DX-capable ham shack, testing antennas and equipment, tweaking as I learn what actually works.

## Thinking About Theory

- Work through licensing material (General → Extra) at a pace that keeps it useful, not stressful.
- Explore Maxwell’s equations and electromagnetic theory through Griffiths and Bertrand:
  - Refresh calculus as needed, then see how the math explains what I notice on the air and in circuits.
  - Treat theory as a tool to make sense of experiments, not as a separate checkbox.

## Tinkering and Experiments

- Use the website as a living lab notebook — record observations, successes, failures, and weird surprises.
- Play with Raspberry Pi and Python to log, visualize, and explore data from experiments.
- Test HF, DC, and electronics circuits: build something, watch what happens, tweak, repeat.
- Let each result spark the next experiment or idea.

The plan is iterative: try, watch, reflect, adjust. Maxwell’s equations are ambitious, antennas may not work perfectly, and not every QSO will succeed — but each rabbit hole ties together, slowly giving a bigger picture of how radio, electronics, and theory interact.
